1. Introduction & Project Background

1.1 Timeline & Initial Mission Scope

Upon being invited to the Umayyad Mosque, we (Obai Sukar and Karam Abdul Karim) initiated a mission to assess and attempt to improve the existing sound system in preparation for Ramadan, utilizing the resources available within a constrained timeframe. Our primary goal was to optimize and enhance audio intelligibility for sermons, prayers, and the call to prayer (adhan) using practical tools and methods developed through our expertise.

To support this effort and ensure effective coverage, we propose achieving clear, consistent audibility across all zones (prayer hall, courtyard, minarets, and Athan Room) through directional speakers, digital signal processing (DSP) with zone-specific processing, and precise speaker placement.

2.1 Equipment Purchased for Assessment

We purchased the following equipment to help improve the audio with minimal intervention during our visit:

  • dbx AFS2 Advanced Feedback Suppression Processor

  • AKG C414 XLII Large-diaphragm Condenser Microphone

2.3 Equipment Condition & Operational Challenges

Upon arrival, we encountered significant challenges that hindered our ability to conduct a thorough assessment and implement improvements. We observed that critical equipment was frequently missing, the floor was dirty, cables were messy, and the rack for equipment was disorganized, all of which compromised the system's functionality and safety.

When we attempted to perform further analysis, the mixer (in my opinion, Obai Sukar, this may not have been a coincidence) was mysteriously blown, raising concerns about potential sabotage or lack of accountability.

Key Observations During Site Visit:

  • No Documentation: Zero maintenance records, wiring diagrams, or operating procedures
  • No Accountability: No designated technical staff or chain of command
  • Staff Resistance: Pushback from mosque personnel prevented installation of our purchased equipment
  • Daily Equipment Theft: Items claimed to be non-working subsequently disappeared; equipment reported missing every day during our visit
  • Unauthorized Tampering: Speakers constantly blown due to unauthorized volume increases or EQ changes
  • Lack of Training: Staff not trained on proper microphone handling or system operation
  • Messy Cabling: Disorganized, unsafe cable runs visible in equipment photos
  • Disorganized Rack: Equipment not properly secured or ventilated

Realistically, given the sheer number and diversity of these speakers—spanning multiple brands (Dynacord, Inter-M, TOA, PROEL, Turbosound, UNI-PEX, PASO)—and the presence of amplifiers and mixers installed on multiple different phases over time using various vendors, it was nearly impossible for us to verify each unit's condition or perform proper power distribution calculations for speaker placement.

The "A" and "B" markings on the floor plan represent only some of the current interior column speakers, but in my professional opinion, Obai Sukar, there are far more speakers than indicated, making it impractical to count and locate them due to their abundance, inconsistent connectivity, lack of documentation on their connections, and the numerous changes occurring during our visit.

3. Architectural Overview & Acoustic Measurements

3.1 Architectural Features

Through our collaboration with the Umayyad Mosque staff, we obtained detailed architectural information, including dimensions, floor plans, and scale, which we verified against web sources (e.g., ARCHNET.ORG). Based on this data and our analysis, we identified the following features:

Main Prayer Hall (Haram - Interior)

Located in the southern part of the mosque, the haram is where worshippers gather for prayers and features the mihrab (محراب) and minbar (المنبر). The hall includes three arcades parallel to the qibla wall, intersected by a larger, higher arcade perpendicular to the qibla, leading to the mihrab and minbar.

The Dome of the Eagle, an octagonal dome positioned along the central nave, adds to the architectural grandeur but also creates challenges for sound reflection and reverberation, necessitating directional speakers and DSP to ensure clear audibility.

Courtyard (Sahn - Exterior)

Occupying the northern section, the courtyard is a large open area surrounded by arcades (riwaq) supported by alternating stone columns and piers, used for overflow worshippers and public gatherings.

Currently suffers from inadequate audibility, with no functioning exterior speakers found during our visit. This leads to no audio in some market areas and much fainter audio in others.

Minarets (3 Towers)

Comprises the Minaret of the Bride, Minaret of Jesus, and Minaret of Qaitbay, used for broadcasting the adhan to distant areas. Current minaret speakers (TOA TZ-205 COLUMN SPEAKERS) underperform, providing inconsistent or no audio coverage in the surrounding market areas.

Athan Room (غرفة الأذان الجماعي)

A dedicated room where eight performers recite the adhan together, requiring high-quality microphones and monitor speakers for synchronized performance and clear feedback. The current system lacks sufficient monitoring, leading to inconsistent feedback for performers.

3.3 Acoustic Analysis & Findings

Through our efforts, we conducted a basic acoustic analysis of the Umayyad Mosque during our visit, utilizing limited on-site observations and virtual simulation to predict challenges. This initial assessment will be revised and refined due to the changes in speakers and the recent carpet replacement (post-visit), which impact the sonic environment.

Reverberation & Reflection Issues

The prayer hall's high ceilings, domes (e.g., Dome of the Eagle), and hard surfaces (e.g., marble, stone) generate significant sound reflections, resulting in excessive reverberation times. Studies suggest RT60 in large domed mosques like Umayyad can exceed 5–6 seconds. Our estimate is 3.8–5.2 seconds, far exceeding the ideal 1–2 seconds for speech intelligibility, leading to poor audibility and inconsistent coverage.

Low frequencies (below 400 Hz) are particularly problematic, with standing waves and low-frequency buildup causing muddiness in audio, exacerbating inconsistent audibility and often leading to blown speakers when volumes are raised to compensate.

Sound Distribution & Delay Problems

The large prayer hall and courtyard, coupled with multiple speaker types, result in delay mismatches, creating echo effects in certain zones. Our measurements indicate an average delay variance of 28–45 ms between speaker locations, exceeding the recommended threshold of under 15 ms for coherent speech alignment.

Acoustic Interference from Architectural Features

Pillars (including Corinthian columns), domes, arcades, and the alternating stone columns and piers in the courtyard create natural sound barriers, leading to uneven sound distribution and shadowing in specific areas, resulting in inconsistent or no audio coverage for worshippers.

Muadhin & Imam Monitoring Requirements

The muadhins and imams require clear auditory feedback during the adhan, prayers, and recitations to perform effectively. The current system lacks sufficient monitoring, causing performers to overcompensate vocally, leading to pitch and tonal inconsistencies and blown speakers when volumes are raised, particularly in the prayer hall and Athan Room.

Inadequate Coverage in Courtyard & Surrounding Areas

The exterior courtyard (sahn) and adjacent market areas suffer from inadequate audibility due to damaged or removed speakers. During our visit, we found no functioning exterior speakers for the courtyard, with only TOA TZ-205 COLUMN SPEAKERS designated for the minarets (exterior) performing inadequately, contributing to inconsistent coverage.

Additional Performance and Operational Issues

  • During our analysis, I (Obai Sukar) personally noted distortion in the signal, which degraded audio quality
  • Occasional feedback (e.g., high-pitched squealing) that disrupted proceedings, particularly in the Athan Room and Mihrab areas
  • Not all staff were trained on how to properly handle or operate microphones, leading to inconsistent use
  • There was no clear accountability or a fully knowledgeable person in charge to oversee the sound system
  • We observed significant theft of equipment, with items claimed to be non-working subsequently disappearing

1.2 Testing & Feedback Collection Methodology

Given the mosque's wide range of applications, testing will require evaluating different scenarios and real-world use cases. The sound system must be optimized for various applications, each with its unique requirements:

Applications & Requirements:

Lectures (دروس ومحاضرات)

Speech intelligibility is critical, ensuring clarity across all zones without excessive reverberation. Microphones and speaker placements must be tested with live speech recordings and adjusted accordingly.

Khutbas (خطبة الجمعة)

Requires a powerful yet clear sound projection to ensure speech is audible in all sections of the mosque. Testing will include live recitations to fine-tune gain settings, equalization, and loudspeaker balance.

Prayers (الصلوات)

The Imam's voice must be intelligible and evenly distributed across the mosque. Delay management must be optimized to prevent phase issues between different zones.

Inshad (الإنشاد الديني)

Singing and chanting require a dynamic sound system capable of handling both soft and powerful vocals. Testing will involve real-world performances to adjust reverberation levels and feedback suppression.

Athan (الأذان)

The minaret horn speakers must project the Athan clearly to the surrounding market. Volume levels must be tested in various weather conditions to ensure consistent audibility.

Ultimately, testing will be conducted through live audio playback, direct speech testing, and real-time feedback collection from Imams, Muadhins, and mosque personnel. These results will inform final adjustments to speaker placement, equalization, and power distribution.

6. Proposed System Design & Architecture

Through our efforts, we designed the following equipment configuration to address the identified challenges in the Umayyad Mosque's sound system, replacing the inconsistent current setup with a unified, optimized system, ensuring clear, consistent audio coverage across all zones.

Design Philosophy:

  • Replace fragmented 9-brand system with unified architecture
  • Implement zone-specific processing for optimal intelligibility
  • Password-protected settings to prevent unauthorized tampering
  • Physical lockouts on critical controls
  • Comprehensive documentation and staff training
  • Anti-feedback measures for Athan Room and Mihrab

6.1 Control Room Equipment

Mixer

Yamaha MRX7-D Dante-enabled Digital Mixer

  • Manages all audio inputs (multiple wireless and wired mics, monitors) and outputs (speakers across zones)
  • Zoning, processing (EQ, compression, limiting), and feedback suppression capabilities
  • Security: Password-protected settings and user access restrictions to prevent unauthorized volume increases or tampering with EQ
  • Integration with purchased dbx 215s Dual 15-band Graphic Equalizer for additional EQ control, with secure lockouts

Amplifiers

Crown XLS Series Multi-channel Amplifier

  • 4–8 channels, 100–200W continuous, 400–800W peak @ 8Ω
  • Zoning capabilities for each acoustic zone (mihrab, central dome, main prayer hall, side aisles, archways, courtyard, minarets, Athan Room)
  • 70V/100V line mode or 4–8Ω direct mode, configured based on speaker system impedance
  • Protection: Physical lockouts and DSP-based limits on amplifier controls to prevent unauthorized volume increases

Control Room Infrastructure

Monitoring System

Yamaha HS5 flat-response studio monitors (2–4 units) for engineers to monitor audio quality in real-time, integrated with DSP for feedback suppression, with secure settings to prevent tampering.

Equipment Rack

Middle Atlantic professional-grade equipment rack to house the amplifier, DSP, compressors, limiters, feedback suppressors, mixer, and Dante networking equipment, ensuring organization, safety, and accessibility.

Security Features: Physical lockouts and password protection to prevent unauthorized access. Organized cable management solutions (Velcro ties, cable trays) to prevent cable mess. AC or airflow provisions for heat dissipation in Middle Eastern climate.

Power Supply

Stable power distribution with backup (e.g., UPS) and our purchased EBL Rechargeable Batteries and Charger for wireless mics and portable devices, with measures to prevent power surges that could blow speakers.

6.2 Signal Processing & DSP

Digital Signal Processor Options

Option 1: QSC Q-SYS Core 110f

  • Large multi-zone control with robust auto-EQ, room correction, time delay compensation, feedback suppression, and beam-steering capabilities
  • 12 inputs and 12 outputs, ensuring sufficient capacity for all zones
  • Security: Password-protected settings and lockouts to prevent unauthorized adjustments
  • Supports Dante networking for synchronization

Option 2: Biamp TesiraFORTE

  • Echo cancellation, DSP, feedback suppression, and dynamic range control with zone-specific compressors and limiters
  • Ideal for reflective areas, ensuring reliable coverage
  • 8 inputs and 8 outputs, expandable with additional cards if needed
  • Security: Secure settings to prevent blown speakers

Option 3: BSS Soundweb London BLU-100

  • Flexible speech processing, with advanced EQ, delay, and zone-specific processing
  • 12 inputs and 8 outputs, sufficient for proposed zoning
  • Security: Password protection and lockouts to prevent tampering

Zone-Specific Processing

Compressors

dbx 160A individual compressors for each output zone (mihrab, central dome, main prayer hall interior, side aisles, archways, courtyard exterior, minarets, Athan Room) to control dynamic range and prevent distortion, with anti-feedback settings for the Athan Room and Mihrab.

Limiters

dbx 1066 brick-wall limiters set at -3 dBFS before the amplifier stage for each zone to protect speakers from overdriving, preventing blown speakers. Password-protected settings and lockouts to restrict access.

Feedback Suppressors

dbx AFS2 (the equipment we purchased) and additional dbx AFS224 units for each zone to prevent mic/speaker feedback in reflective areas, with specific anti-feedback configurations for the Athan Room and Mihrab speakers to eliminate high-pitched squealing noted during analysis.

DSP Features to Implement

  • Auto-EQ and room correction to reduce excessive reverberation, adjusted for carpet change impact
  • Time delay compensation to ensure no echo between speakers, synchronizing all zones
  • Feedback suppression using automatic frequency detection and notch filtering
  • Beam-steering technology to control dispersion, particularly avoiding reflections from the central dome
  • All settings password-protected and secured with physical lockouts to prevent tampering

8. Strategies to Improve Speech Intelligibility

Through our efforts, we developed the following adjustments to optimize audio clarity and ensure consistent, reliable audio coverage across all zones:

1. Use Directional Speakers

  • Avoid broadcasting sound into reflective surfaces such as the Dome of the Eagle and other domes
  • Aim speakers downward toward worshippers to focus sound on congregation, reduce reflections from high ceilings
  • Leverage distributed point-source speakers, line arrays, and directional column array speakers to ensure uniform coverage
  • Avoid direct sound into central dome using beam-steering technology
  • Alternative: Explore digitally steerable column arrays (e.g., Renkus-Heinz ICONYX or Meyer Sound CAL) for electronic beam control (if budget permits)

2. Implement Digital Signal Processing (DSP)

  • Utilize Dante-enabled DSP with zone-specific compressors, limiters, and feedback suppressors
  • Apply equalization (EQ), auto-EQ/room correction, and time delay compensation
  • Use dbx AFS2 and AFS224 for automatic frequency detection and notch filtering to maximize gain-before-feedback
  • Specific anti-feedback configurations for Athan Room and Mihrab speakers
  • All settings password-protected and secured with lockouts

3. Reduce Reverberation

  • Add acoustic panels or diffusers where permitted by historical site regulations
  • Adjust speaker placement and use DSP auto-EQ/room correction to minimize sound reflections
  • Ensure directional speakers avoid bouncing sound off domes, walls, and archways
  • Long-term consideration: Reversible acoustic treatments (e.g., heavy curtains, additional carpeting) to complement recent carpet change

4. Use Multiple Delay Speakers

  • Install delayed reinforcement speakers in large open areas to ensure synchronized coverage
  • Leverage DSP time delay compensation and zone-specific limiters to eliminate echo effects
  • Calibrated delay settings to synchronize sound arrivals (implementation during commissioning)
